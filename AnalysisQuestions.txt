1. Fragmentation Analysis: In the basic example, what is the external fragmentation percentage? Explain why free memory becomes fragmented. 

      50 to 99 = 50, 500 to 1023 = 524 
      50 + 524 = 574 
      100(Total-largest)/total = 100(574-524)/574 =~ 8.7% 

      Free memory becomes fragmented because memory is allocated and released over time. 
      When a process is freed, it leaves behind its space in memory, which other processes can use. 
      However, the next process may not ‘fit’ the same; it may only partially fill the available space. 
      This results in multiple separated free blocks, instead of one large continuous block, which limits the amount of processing that uses the memory.   

2. First-Fit Efficiency: Why does P4 use the block left by P1 even though it's larger than needed? What happens to the unused portion? 

      P4 uses the block left by P1 even though it is larger than needed because First-Fit allocation selects 
      the first available block that is large enough, without searching for a better option. First-Fit prioritizes 
      speed over space efficiency, unlike Best-Fit, which focuses on minimizing wasted space. The unused portion of 
      the block is split off into a smaller free block that future processes can use. 

3. Allocation Patterns: Compare workload1.txt (many small processes) with workload2.txt (few large processes). Which creates more fragmentation? Why? 

      Workload1.txt creates more fragmentation because frequent allocations and small processes create and use many 
      small gaps in memory, leaving behind free spaces that are most likely to be too small for other processes to use. 
      Workload2.txt has fewer, larger allocations, so memory is divided into larger blocks with fewer gaps and less fragmentation. 

4. Failed Allocations: Create a scenario where an allocation fails even though the total free memory is larger than the request. Why does this happen? 

	    Given a total memory of 300KB, there are two free blocks of 200KB and 100KB. A process needs 250KB of memory.   

	    In this scenario, allocation fails because, even though we have 300KB of total free memory, there are no single blocks 
      large enough to handle the 250KB process. Over time, memory is split as processes are allocated and released at different points in memory.  
      These split pieces do not re-combine and remain separate. Thus, even though the total amount of free space is greater than our process size, 
      no block is large enough for it. 

5. Algorithm Comparison: How might Best-Fit allocation handle the basic example differently than First-Fit? Would it reduce fragmentation? 

	    Best-Fit allocation would handle the basic example by selecting the smallest free block that meets the process's required size. 
      Unlike first-fit, which chooses the first block it finds that is large enough, Best-fit traverses all free blocks to find the most 
      efficient fit for the process. In the basic example we are given, First-Fit and Best-fit result in the same placement of P4.   

    	Generally, Best-fit can reduce wasted space because larger blocks would remain available for larger processes. However, it can still 
      increase fragmentation because repeatedly splitting smaller blocks will eventually create small leftover memory spaces that may not be 
      useful to future processes.
